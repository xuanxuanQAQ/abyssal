## Task: General Literature Analysis (Exploratory Mode)

The researcher has not yet defined a conceptual framework — they are in the early exploration phase of their research. Your task is fundamentally different from concept-anchored analysis:

**You are NOT mapping the paper to existing concepts.** Instead, you are helping the researcher discover what concepts might be relevant to their research.

### Your Tasks

1. **Summary** (200-300 words): Summarize the paper's main contributions, situating them within the broader field. Focus on what is novel or distinctive about this paper's approach.

2. **Key Arguments**: Identify the paper's central claims. For each claim:
   - State the claim in one sentence.
   - Describe the evidence type (empirical / theoretical / anecdotal).
   - Assess the strength of the evidence (strong / moderate / weak).

3. **Methodological Assessment**: Evaluate the research methodology:
   - Design appropriateness for the research questions.
   - Sample adequacy (if applicable).
   - Key validity concerns.

4. **Critical Evaluation**: What does this paper do well? Where does it fall short? Identify at least 2 substantive limitations (not the authors' self-reported caveats, but your own assessment).

5. **Key Concept Discovery** ★ THIS IS YOUR MOST IMPORTANT TASK ★
   Identify 3-5 key concepts or terms that appear central to this paper's theoretical or empirical contribution. For each:
   - **term**: The concept name as used in the paper.
   - **frequency_in_paper**: Approximate number of times it appears.
   - **reason**: Why this concept is significant (1-2 sentences).
   - **suggested_definition**: A working definition based on how the paper uses this term (1-2 sentences).
   - **suggested_keywords**: 3-5 related search terms for finding more literature on this concept.
   - **closest_existing**: If this concept resembles any well-known theoretical construct, name it.

   Focus on concepts that are:
   - Used as analytical tools (not just mentioned in passing).
   - Defined or operationalized by the authors.
   - Central to the paper's argument or methodology.
   - Potentially useful as organizing principles for a research framework.
